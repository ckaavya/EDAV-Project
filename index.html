Introduction

NYC is home to more than 8 million people and sees roughly 50 million tourists every year. Being one of the world’s major commercial hubs, there is a lot of traffic on both the roads and subways, the two most popular means of commuting, for most parts of the day. As a result of continuously intensifying interactions as population increases , more frequent accidents on the roads are expected.
NYC Open Data provides easy access to a lot of data pertaining to motor vehicle accidents on the streets of New York for the time period 2012 -2017. There have been several attempts to study, explore and visualise this data. However, we found that not many currently provide any deep insights for making comparisons or for obtaining shifting/emerging trends over the years. The aim of our project is to develop a more comprehensive exploratory visualization tool to enable users to explore more aspects of the data and enable them to make better inferences.

Monthly Trends

The chart above shows that February is the safest month with least no. of accidents whereas May and October were the unsafest months with maximum accidents recorded. This could be mainly due to the seasonality trends. Extreme snow conditions occur in the month of February whereas the summer season (May-October) usually offers the best weather and driving conditions of the year – dry roads, excellent visibility, and longer daylight hours. This leads to several more cars on the roads and consequently, more accidents due to the additional traffic.

The period between Memorial Day and Labor Day – summer vacation for most students – has been called "The 100 Deadliest Days" for teen drivers. Nine of the 10 deadliest days for youth on U.S. highways fall between May and August.

Rush hours

No. of accidents vary in a big way with time of day. During the early morning hours (12AM - 7AM), traffic on roads is much lesser which accounts for lesser accidents. Also early in the morning, people are more attentive and there are fewer collisions due to driver inattention. The afternoon rush hours between 2-6 PM play host to most crashes as this is the time when commuters travel back home, to eat, spend time with families, and/or get to work on a second job. After rush hour in the evening once people travel back home from work, traffic intensity and accidents go down again.

Weekday vs Weekend

The heatmap above shows count of accident by day of the week and time. As expected, more accidents around 2-6 PM occur more during the weekdays than weekends.

However, if we look at late hours around 12-4 AM Saturday and Sunday is the most dangerous day to drive primarily because there are more drunk drivers on the road than any other day.

Deaths

The map shows every death caused by a motor vehicle collision in NYC during 2013-16. Every death is represented as a dot colored by type. The map is cumulative, hence one can observe the colors of the dots changing over time. Pedestrian deaths are shown in red, motorist deaths in blue and cyclist deaths in yellow. A very first glance shows that pedestrian and motorist deaths are far more in number as compared to cyclist deaths. This is very intuitive as majority of people on roads are motorists and pedestrians in NYC
